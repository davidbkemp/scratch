Multiple qubits

Important: There is something really important I feel the need to emphasize as we start dealing with multiple qubits.

The blue disks introduced in Part 1 do not correspond to single qubits.

Each blue disk corresponds to a distinct state of the entire quantum system. The blue disks are technically called state “amplitudes” and are represented mathematically using complex numbers.

Controlled NOT

The Controlled NOT operator is a very simple but powerful two-qubit operator.

The Controlled NOT operator modifies the state of one qubit (the target qubit) based on state of another qubit (the control qubit).

Specifically, the Controlled NOT operator flips the value of the target qubit if the control qubit has a value of 1.

For example: If the initial state is "11", and if the left qubit is used as the control qubit, and the right qubit is the target, then the result will be "10". As usual, we represent this as “11 → 10”

For the same choice of control and target qubits (left qubit as control qubit and right qubit as target):

  • 00 → 00 (i.e. no effect)
  • 01 → 01 (i.e. no effect)
  • 10 → 11 (i.e. target bit flips)
  • 11 → 10 (i.e. target bit flips)

Quantum Entanglement

A combination of the Hadamard followed by a Controlled NOT can put two qubits into a state of “quantum entanglement”.

In what follows, we take a two qubit system in the state "00", apply the Hadamard operation to the left qubit, and then use that left qubit as the control in a Controlled NOT of the right qubit:

Initial State

State: 100% chance of being 00

After applying a Hadamard to the left qubit

State: 50% chance of being 00, and 50% chance of being 10

After a Controlled NOT of the right qubit controlled by the left qubit

State: 50% chance of being 00, and 50% chance of being 11

Note that, if you were to measure the qubits while they are in this state, you would find that they are either both 0 or both 1.

Y Rotations

There are three particlar single qubit operators commonly referred to as the X, Y, and Z Rotation operators. Historically, these refer to physical rotations in three dimensional space around the X, Y, and Z axes.

For this article we will only make use of the Y Rotation operator.

Like all the rotation operators, you need to specify how many degrees of rotation you want applied.

A 90° Y rotation is a little bit like a Hadamard: It transforms a pure 0 state (or pure 1 state) into an equal super-position of 0 and 1.

A 180° Y rotation is a little bit like a NOT: it flips a 0 into a 1 and a 1 into 0 except that, when flipping from 1 to 0, it also flips the phase.

As expected, two 90° Y rotations gives a 180° Y rotation etc. Strangely, a 360° Y rotation, while leaving a 0 state as 0 (and a 1 state as 1), it actually flips the phase.

Did you notice?

Did you notice that a 45° Y rotation of a 0 state resulted in a state with a 85% chance of 0 and 15% chance of 1? This will become important later.

Given that 45° is half of 90°, and given that a 90° Y rotation of a 0 state results in a state with a 50% chance of 0 and 50% chance of 1, you might have expected a 45° Y rotation to take you half way: i.e. 75% chance of a 0 and 25% chance of a 1.

But that is not how rotations work. If you rotate a circle by 90°, the bottom of the circle will be rotated to a height halfway up to the top. But if you only rotate it by 45°, the bottom of the circle will be rotated to a height significantly less than a quarter of the height of the circle. The quantum mechanics of Y Rotations actually means that a rotation by θ of a 0 state results in a state with a cos2(θ) chance of remaining 0 and a sin2(θ) of becoming a 1.
